Duke University · POLSCI 719 · State Formation

A Genealogy of State Formation

A semester of state-formation readings reorganized into one genealogy: a five-segment problem chain running across four analytic lanes and six historical stages, where each new cluster of work answers the question the previous one could not.

What this genealogy is

State formation is not only the history of a violence monopolist expanding extraction; it is the history of turning populations and resources into objects that are visible, taxable, governable, credibly committed to, and capable of cooperation. The course runs a five-segment problem chain — appropriability → war & coercion → agency & monitoring → commitment & compliance → long-run consequences.

Four vertical lanes mark where each work does its causal work — material–technological, coercion–extraction, organization–representation, justification–belief — and six horizontal stages set its historical setting, from pristine states to the present. The genealogy is one structure viewed several ways: switch lenses to recolor the same nodes by method, by legitimacy, or by their relation to the bellicist (Tilly) paradigm. Nothing moves between lenses; only the coloring and emphasis change.

The justification–belief lane (rightmost) is sparse in this course, but not empty: normative-legitimacy nodes appear in weeks 11–12 (mass schooling, the Investiture Controversy, the Reformation). It may later be joined by nodes carried over from other courses that treat legitimacy head-on.
