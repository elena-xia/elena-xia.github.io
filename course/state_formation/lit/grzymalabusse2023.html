Dependent variable (DV)

The main dependent variable across these two chapters is European state formation, but this is not a single, uniformly operationalized outcome.

In Chapter 2, the most clearly measured dependent variable is territorial fragmentation, operationalized as the number of state boundaries within a 100km × 100km grid cell across Europe from 1000 to 1800 (pp. 62–67). A second, more limited dependent variable is the rise of self-governing communes (pp. 67–68). Chapter 2 also discusses the differentiation of secular and ecclesiastical authority and the early articulation of sovereignty, but these are better understood as historical-conceptual outcomes of papal-imperial rivalry than as dependent variables measured at the same level as the regression-based fragmentation outcome.

In Chapter 3, the dependent variable shifts again, this time to the development of governing institutions: the emergence, timing, and form of chanceries, treasuries/cameras, taxation systems, courts, assemblies, and censuses across European polities (pp. 75–81). Thus, across the two chapters, “state formation” is treated as a family of related outcomes rather than one single operationalized DV.

这两章的核心因变量可以概括为欧洲国家形成（state formation），但它并不是一个单一、统一操作化的结果变量。

在第二章中，最清楚、最正式被测量的因变量是领土碎片化，具体操作化为1000–1800年间欧洲每个 100km × 100km 网格中的国家边界数量（第62–67页）。第二个较次要的因变量是自治公社的兴起（第67–68页）。第二章也讨论了世俗权威与教会权威的分化以及主权概念的早期形成，但它们更适合被理解为教皇，帝国竞争带来的历史，概念性后果，而不是与“碎片化”处于同一层次、可直接进入回归模型的因变量。

到了第三章，因变量再次发生变化，转向治理制度的发展：即文书处、财务署/国库、税收、法院、议会和人口普查等制度在各欧洲政治体中的出现时间、制度形式与发展顺序（第75–81页）。因此，这两章中的“国家形成”更准确地说是一组相互关联但并不统一操作化的结果，而不是一个单一 DV。

Independent variable (IV)

The broad independent variable is the medieval Catholic Church, especially the political actions and institutional innovations of the papacy. But it is operationalized differently across the two chapters.

In Chapter 2, the key IV is papal conflict with secular rulers, defined as wars by proxy against papal enemies, papal depositions of rulers, and military attacks led or financed by popes directly (p. 62). This is the clearest case of a directly coded explanatory variable.

In Chapter 3, however, the argument is not best described as a single IV called “institutional isomorphism.” More precisely, the explanatory claim is that the Church developed administrative, fiscal, and judicial templates earlier than royal courts, and these templates were subsequently adapted by secular rulers. “Institutional isomorphism” is the author’s theoretical language for this process of adaptation and borrowing, but empirically the chapter focuses on the prior existence of papal institutions, their transmission through clergy and legal-administrative networks, and their later uptake by royal courts. So in Chapter 3, the Church’s prior institutional development is the substantive explanatory factor, while “isomorphism” is better understood as the mechanism or interpretive framework than as a cleanly operationalized IV.

广义上的自变量是中世纪天主教会，尤其是教皇的政治行动与制度创新。但它在两章中的操作化方式并不相同。

在第二章中，关键自变量是教皇与世俗统治者之间的冲突（papal conflict），其定义包括：教皇通过盟友发动的代理战争、对统治者的废黜、以及由教皇直接领导或资助的军事攻击（第62页）。这是全书中最接近直接编码型解释变量的部分。

但在第三章中，作者的解释不宜简单写成一个名叫“制度同构”（institutional isomorphism）的单一 IV。更准确地说，作者的解释是：教会比王室政体更早发展出行政、财政和司法制度模板，而世俗统治者后来吸收、改造并移植了这些模板。“制度同构”是作者用来描述这种借用和适应过程的理论语言，但就经验分析而言，第三章真正关注的是教廷制度的先行发展、这些制度通过神职人员和法律，行政网络的传播，以及王室宫廷后来的采纳。因此，在第三章中，更合适的写法是：教会既有制度发展是解释性因素，而“制度同构”更像机制或解释框架，而不是一个被清楚操作化的单一自变量。

Stateness

Grzymala-Busse explicitly acknowledges that “state institutions” in the medieval context did not resemble modern states. Medieval rulers defended fuzzy borders, raised limited revenue, and adjudicated disputes; they did not supply public goods like education or healthcare (pp. 77–78). She characterizes medieval governance as marked by “weak capacity, lack of specificity, and contested sovereignty” (p. 78). The “state” is thus conceptualized not as a coherent Weberian bureaucracy but as a cluster of governing offices and capacities that gradually differentiated and specialized.

In Chapter 2, stateness is measured via (a) the degree of territorial fragmentation (number of state boundaries per grid cell, using Abramson’s [2017] shape files and criteria for defining “states” as principalities, duchies, baronies, and cities that can tax and have a common executive [p. 55]); (b) the presence of self-governing communes (Van Zanden, Buringh, and Bosker 2012); and (c) qualitative indicators of the differentiation of secular from religious authority and the emergence of sovereignty concepts.

In Chapter 3, stateness is measured via the timing and institutional form of six state institutions, chanceries, cameras/treasuries, taxation, legal courts, national assemblies, and censuses, across the Church, England, France, Spain, Italy, Sweden, Poland, and the Holy Roman Empire (Figures 3.1 and 3.2, pp. 80–81). This measurement is essentially descriptive-comparative rather than fully formalized or uniform: it relies on secondary historical literature to identify when institutions emerged and how they were organized. So although the chapter does “measure” state development, it does so in a broad historical-comparative sense, not through a common quantitative measurement model.

明确承认，中世纪语境下的“国家制度”与现代国家截然不同。中世纪统治者守卫的是模糊的边界，征收有限的收入，裁决纠纷；他们并不提供教育或医疗等公共产品（第77–78页）。她将中世纪治理的特征概括为“能力薄弱、定义不清、主权争议”（第78页）。因此，“国家”在此被概念化为逐渐分化和专门化的一系列治理机构和能力的集合，而非韦伯式的内聚性官僚体系。

在第二章中，国家性通过以下指标衡量：（a）领土碎片化程度（每个网格单元中的国家边界数量，使用Abramson [2017年]的地理信息数据和对“国家”的界定标准，即能够征税并拥有共同行政长官的公国、公爵领、男爵领和城市 [第55页]）；（b）自治公社的存在（Van Zanden, Buringh, and Bosker 2012年）；（c）世俗权威与宗教权威分化以及主权概念萌芽的定性指标。

在第三章中，国家性通过六项国家制度，文书处、财务署/国库、税收、法院、国民议会和人口普查，在教会、英格兰、法兰西、西班牙、意大利、瑞典 /ˈswiːdən/、波兰 /ˈpoʊlənd/ 和神圣罗马帝国 /ˌhoʊli ˈroʊmən ˈɛmpaɪr/ 的出现时间和制度形式来衡量（图3.1和3.2，第80–81页）。但这种“测量”本质上是描述性，比较性的，而不是完全统一、形式化的测量模型：它主要依赖二手历史文献来判断制度何时出现、以何种形式被组织起来。所以，第三章确实在衡量国家发展，但这是宽意义上的历史比较式“测量”，而不是统一量化指标意义上的 measurement。

Core argument

The overarching argument is that the medieval Catholic Church was a primary driver of European state formation, and it operated through two distinct mechanisms elaborated in these chapters: rivalry (Chapter 2) and emulation (Chapter 3).

In Chapter 2, the argument is that the papacy’s struggle for autonomy and its determination to prevent any hegemon, especially the Hohenstaufen /ˈhoʊənʃtaʊfən/ Holy Roman Emperors, from encircling the Papal States led to a deliberate policy of fragmenting territorial authority in Europe. The papacy wielded both spiritual weapons (excommunications, interdicts) and temporal strategies (coalitions, proxy wars, crusades) to break apart the Empire and prevent consolidation. This papal fragmentation, concentrated in the 12th and 13th centuries, was not evenly distributed: it was most intense where rulers clashed most with the pope, in the German and Italian lands, and least where the papacy needed allies (England, France, Spain) (pp. 55–57). A critical consequence was that the territories most embroiled in papal conflict (Germany, Italy) remained fragmented until the 1870s, while those spared (England, France, Spain) consolidated earlier. The author explicitly challenges the dominant “bellicist” narrative (Tilly 1992; Hintze), which holds that early modern interstate warfare winnowed states into large, consolidated units. She argues instead that medieval religious conflict, not early modern secular warfare, explains the initial and persistent pattern of European fragmentation (pp. 56, 67, 74). Moreover, the papacy’s articulation of sovereignty concepts, the rex in regno suo imperator est doctrine, eventually backfired, as secular rulers used these same concepts to reject papal supremacy (pp. 69–71). The Great Schism (1378–1417) then decisively weakened the papacy and empowered secular states (pp. 71–74).

In Chapter 3, the argument is that the papacy’s institutional innovations, in chanceries, taxation, and judicial administration, served as templates that royal courts emulated. The church was the first centralized administration in Europe; its Curia developed specialized offices (Chancery, Camera, Rota) a century or more before secular counterparts (pp. 79–83). These innovations traveled to royal courts primarily through clergy who served simultaneously as church officials and royal administrators, creating a shared epistemic and institutional culture (pp. 76–77, 86–89). The author shows that the English Exchequer arose under a bishop, that crusade taxation (especially the Saladin Tithe of 1188) provided the model for direct per-capita taxation by secular rulers, and that the papal petition-and-appeal system shaped royal judiciary development (pp. 91–98, 103–106). However, this process was uneven: in the Holy Roman Empire, where papal fragmentation had gutted central authority, central state institutions barely developed until 1500 (pp. 107–110). The irony was that the very administrative capacities the church provided ultimately made the church itself superfluous, as lay officials gradually displaced clerics (pp. 110–112).

总体论点是：中世纪天主教会是欧洲国家形成的主要推动力量，其作用通过两章中阐述的两个不同机制，竞争（第二章）和效仿（第三章），来实现。

第二章的论点是：教皇为争取自治权，并防止任何霸权，尤其是霍亨施陶芬 /ˈhoʊənʃtaʊfən/ 王朝的神圣罗马帝国皇帝，包围教皇国，采取了蓄意碎片化欧洲领土权威的政策。教皇运用精神武器（绝罚、禁教令）和世俗策略（联盟、代理战争、十字军东征）来瓦解帝国并阻止其整合。这种教皇主导的碎片化集中于12–13世纪，且分布不均：在统治者与教皇冲突最激烈的地方（德意志和意大利领地）最为剧烈，而在教皇需要盟友的地方（英格兰、法兰西、西班牙）最为温和（第55–57页）。关键后果是：与教皇冲突最深的领土（德意志、意大利）一直保持碎片化直到19世纪70年代，而幸免者（英格兰、法兰西、西班牙）则较早实现整合。作者明确挑战了占主导地位的“战争主义”（bellicist）叙事（Tilly 1992; Hintze），后者认为早期现代国家间战争淘汰出大型统一国家。她则论证，解释欧洲碎片化初始格局和持久性的是中世纪宗教冲突，而非早期现代世俗战争（第56、67、74页）。此外，教皇对主权概念的表述，“国王在其王国内即为皇帝”（rex in regno suo imperator est）这一学说，最终适得其反，因为世俗统治者利用同一概念拒绝了教皇的最高权威（第69–71页）。大分裂（Great Schism, 1378–1417）则决定性地削弱了教皇权力并增强了世俗国家（第71–74页）。

第三章的论点是：教皇的制度创新，在文书处、税收和司法行政方面，作为模板被王室宫廷效仿。教会是欧洲第一个集中化行政机构；其教廷发展出专门化的部门（文书处、财务署、圣轮法院），比世俗对应机构早一个世纪甚至更多（第79–83页）。这些创新主要通过同时担任教会官员和王室行政人员的神职人员传播到王室宫廷，形成了共同的知识和制度文化（第76–77、86–89页）。作者展示了英格兰的财政署（Exchequer）在一位主教领导下建立，十字军税收（尤其是1188年的萨拉丁什一税）为世俗统治者的直接人头税提供了模板，教皇的请愿-上诉体系塑造了王室司法的发展（第91–98、103–106页）。然而，这一过程是不均匀的：在教皇碎片化已经掏空中央权威的神圣罗马帝国，中央国家制度几乎直到1500年才开始发展（第107–110页）。讽刺的是，教会所提供的行政能力最终使教会自身变得多余，因为世俗官员逐渐取代了神职人员（第110–112页）。

Mechanisms

The author identifies two overarching mechanisms and several sub-mechanisms:

Mechanism 1: Rivalry and Fragmentation (Chapter 2). The papacy pursued its institutional self-interest, autonomy from secular rulers, through strategies that fragmented Europe.

Spiritual weapons: Excommunications destabilized rulers, particularly younger and more vulnerable ones (Kaplan-Meier survival analysis, Figure 2.3, p. 47), but lost their edge through overuse by the 14th century (p. 46). Interdicts placed entire communities under religious sanctions. However, excommunications were not decisive: they did not consistently end rulers’ tenure, and powerful monarchs like Henry IV /ˈhɛnri/ (excommunicated five times) and Frederick II /ˈfrɛdərɪk/ survived them for decades (pp. 44–46).

Temporal weapons, coalitions and proxy wars: The papacy allied opportunistically with secular rulers (Normans /ˈnɔːrmənz/, Lombard League /ˈlɒmbɑːrd/, Angevins /ˈændʒɪvɪnz/, etc.), shifting alliances as the balance of power changed (pp. 47–49). Popes funded counter-weights to the Empire, sold invasion rights, and balanced threats from multiple directions.

Crusades as political weapons: Crusade privileges were increasingly extended beyond campaigns against infidels to campaigns against the papacy’s secular enemies, including Frederick II, Manfred /ˈmænfrɛd/, the Colonna /kɒˈlɒnə/ family, and even Flanders /ˈflændərz/ (pp. 49–51).

Empowering sub-units: The power vacuum created by papal-imperial conflict enabled cities and communes to gain autonomy. Popes and emperors granted charters to curry local support (pp. 67–68). Bishops diffused norms of self-governance. The “communal revolution” of the late 11th century grew directly in the shadow of the Investiture Conflict (p. 58).

Conceptual innovation, sovereignty: Canonists articulated the rex in regno suo doctrine to undercut imperial claims, asserting that kings were not subordinate to the emperor. This concept was then turned against the papacy itself, as secular rulers claimed independence from papal supremacy as well (pp. 69–71).

Mechanism 2: Emulation / Institutional Isomorphism (Chapter 3). royal courts adapted and borrowed from church institutional templates through clerical and legal-administrative networks.

Human capital transmission via clergy: Bishops, legates, and clerical officials served simultaneously at the papal curia and royal courts, carrying administrative models, legal training, and Latin literacy across Europe (pp. 76–77, 84–89). The term “clerk” itself came to mean both ecclesiastic and literate official (p. 76).

Chancery templates: The Papal Chancery developed systematic petition-response systems, record-keeping (registers), and standardized writing styles (ars dictaminis, cursus Romae curiae). Royal chanceries adopted these formulas, phrasings, and procedures. English bishops in the 1140s were already copying papal document handwriting and invocations (pp. 86–89).

Taxation templates: The Camera Apostolica pioneered financial record-keeping (Liber Censuum, 1192), direct per-capita taxation (crusade taxes from 1188), and compliance techniques (itinerant tax collector teams, profit-sharing with secular rulers). The Saladin Tithe of 1188 was “a turning point in the development of non-feudal taxation” (Lunt, cited p. 93), introducing assessment based on movable property rather than land, and collection by traveling boards. English kings directly copied these methods for the 1207 lay tax and subsequent levies (pp. 91–98).

Judicial templates: The Rota, Penitentiary, and Signatura provided models for specialized courts. Secular and papal courts expanded in parallel in the 1170s (Common Pleas and the Rota). Kings adopted the appeals system modeled on papal judges-delegate. Competition between ecclesiastical and secular courts also spurred royal judicial development, as kings sought to recapture court fees and authority (pp. 103–107).

Sale of offices: The papacy institutionalized venality after the Great Schism, and French and Spanish monarchs adopted virtually identical contractual terms for selling offices by the mid-15th century (pp. 101–103).

机制一：竞争与碎片化（第二章）。 教皇追求制度性自身利益，摆脱世俗统治者控制，的策略导致了欧洲的碎片化。

精神武器： 绝罚动摇了统治者，尤其是年轻而脆弱的统治者（Kaplan-Meier生存分析，图2.3，第47页），但因过度使用到14世纪已丧失威力（第46页）。禁教令对整个社区施加宗教制裁。然而，绝罚并非决定性的：它不能持续终结统治者的任期，亨利四世 /ˈhɛnri/（被绝罚五次）和腓特烈二世 /ˈfrɛdərɪk/ 等强大的君主在绝罚后仍统治了数十年（第44–46页）。

世俗武器，联盟与代理战争： 教皇机会主义地与世俗统治者结盟（诺曼人 /ˈnɔːrmənz/、伦巴第联盟 /ˈlɒmbɑːrd/、安茹王朝 /ˈændʒɪvɪnz/ 等），随权力平衡变化不断转换联盟（第47–49页）。教皇资助帝国的制衡力量，出售入侵权利，从多个方向平衡威胁。

十字军作为政治武器： 十字军特权日益扩展到针对教皇世俗敌人而非仅仅异教徒的战役，针对腓特烈二世、曼弗雷德 /ˈmænfrɛd/、科隆纳 /kɒˈlɒnə/ 家族，甚至佛兰德斯 /ˈflændərz/（第49–51页）。

赋权次级单位： 教皇-帝国冲突造成的权力真空使城市和公社获得了自治。教皇和皇帝授予特许状以争取地方支持（第67–68页）。主教传播了自治规范。11世纪晚期的“公社革命”直接在叙任权之争的阴影下成长（第58页）。

概念创新，主权： 教会法学家阐述了“国王在其王国内即为皇帝”（rex in regno suo）学说以削弱帝国主张，宣称国王不从属于皇帝。这一概念随后被世俗统治者用来反对教皇，宣称独立于教皇最高权威（第69–71页）。

机制二：效仿/制度同构（第三章）。 世俗统治者采纳了教会的制度模板。

通过神职人员传递人力资本： 主教、教廷使节和神职官员同时在教廷和王室宫廷任职，将行政模式、法律训练和拉丁文读写能力传播到全欧洲（第76–77、84–89页）。“clerk”一词本身就兼有教士和识字官员之意（第76页）。

文书处模板： 教皇文书处发展出系统化的请愿-回复体系、档案管理（登记簿）和标准化书写风格（ars dictaminis, cursus Romae curiae）。王室文书处采纳了这些公式、措辞和程序。英格兰主教在1140年代就已模仿教皇文件的书写和格式（第86–89页）。

税收模板： 宗座财务署开创了财务记录（Liber Censuum, 1192年）、直接人头税（1188年起的十字军税）和合规技术（巡回征税团队、与世俗统治者分享收入）。1188年的萨拉丁什一税是“非封建税收发展的转折点”（Lunt, 引自第93页），引入了基于动产而非土地的评估，以及由巡回委员会征收的方式。英格兰国王直接模仿这些方法实施了1207年世俗税和后续征税（第91–98页）。

司法模板： 圣轮法院（Rota）、忏悔院（Penitentiary）和签署法院（Signatura）为专门法院提供了模式。世俗法院和教皇法院在1170年代并行扩展（普通诉讼法院和圣轮法院）。国王采纳了以教皇委任法官（judges-delegate）为模式的上诉体系。教会法院与世俗法院之间的竞争也刺激了王室司法的发展，因为国王力图夺回法院费用和管辖权（第103–107页）。

卖官鬻爵： 大分裂后教皇将卖官制度化，法兰西和西班牙君主在15世纪中叶几乎照搬了相同的合同条款来出售官职（第101–103页）。

Data & methods

Data

The study uses an extensive combination of quantitative and qualitative data:

Chapter 2, Quantitative:

Territorial fragmentation: State boundary data from Abramson (2017) shape files, augmented by 1900 European country borders from the Mosaic historical maps project. Unit of analysis: 100km × 100km grid cells across Europe. Time period: 1000–1800, divided into medieval (1000–1350) and early modern (1500–1800) sub-periods (pp. 55, 62–63).

Papal conflict: Coded from The New Cambridge Medieval History (1998–2009) and Dupuy and Dupuy (1993). Defined as wars by proxy, depositions, and attacks led/financed by popes (p. 62).

Secular conflict: Share of cities experiencing siege, battle, or conflict, from Dincecco and Onorato (2016) (p. 62).

Excommunication data: Over 100 political excommunications from the 3rd to 20th centuries, coded from the Catholic Encyclopedia and New Cambridge Medieval History. Duration of rule data from Bosworth (1996) and Morby (1989), augmented with Eastern European dynasties (pp. 44–45).

Control variables: Parliaments (Van Zanden, Buringh, and Bosker 2012), urbanization/city population (Bairoch, Batou, and Pierre 1988), communes (Van Zanden et al. 2012), primogeniture (Kokkonen and Sundell 2014), Holy Roman Empire membership, Protestant status (pp. 64–65).

Communes as DV: Presence of communes from Van Zanden et al. (2012), presence of bishoprics (pp. 67–68).

Methods

The methodological approach is mixed, combining quantitative panel regressions with extensive qualitative-historical analysis.

Chapter 2, Quantitative methods:

OLS regressions with two-way fixed effects (cell and year fixed effects), using the 100km × 100km grid cell as the unit of analysis (pp. 62–63). The fixed effects control for time-invariant spatial factors (climate, elevation, agricultural suitability, proximity to ports) and space-invariant temporal shocks.

The sample is split into medieval (1000–1350) and early modern (1500–1800) periods to capture the theorized difference in papal versus secular influence.

The main result (Table 2.1, p. 66) shows that papal conflict is statistically significantly and positively associated with territorial fragmentation in the medieval period across multiple specifications (models 2–4), surviving the inclusion of controls for parliaments, urbanization, communes, and HRE membership. Primogeniture absorbs the papal conflict effect (model 5), but the author argues primogeniture is itself a downstream consequence of church family law reforms, making it a mediator rather than a confounder (p. 65; mediation analysis in appendix table A.5 suggests 33% of the effect is mediated).

In the early modern period, papal conflict is collinear with Protestant status and drops out (models 7–10); secular conflict becomes positively associated with fragmentation, the opposite of what the bellicist thesis predicts (p. 65).

Oster (2019) sensitivity tests \((\delta = 0 = \beta)\) are reported to assess vulnerability to omitted variable bias. The negative values for medieval papal conflict suggest unobservables would need to run in the opposite direction to overturn the results (Table 2.1, p. 66).

Kaplan-Meier survival analysis for excommunicated vs. non-excommunicated rulers (Figure 2.3, p. 47).

A secondary regression uses communes as the DV with the same grid cell structure and finds papal conflict and fragmentation positively predict communes (Figure 2.8, p. 69).

Key assumptions for validity:

Two-way fixed effects require that the relationship between papal conflict and fragmentation is not driven by unobserved time-varying, cell-specific confounders. This is the standard parallel trends-type assumption.

The author acknowledges the reverse causality concern, that popes targeted already-fragmented areas, but argues against it on historical grounds: popes targeted their biggest threats (powerful rulers), and fragmentation increased after papal efforts began in the 12th century, not before (p. 63, footnote 23).

The operationalization of “papal conflict” must be exogenous conditional on the fixed effects and controls. Since popes targeted specific rulers based on geopolitical threat, there is a potential selection-into-treatment concern.

Chapter 3, Qualitative methods:

Structured comparison of institutional development timelines across seven polities and the Church (Figures 3.1, 3.2).

Process tracing of specific causal mechanisms: how the Saladin Tithe shaped English taxation, how clerical personnel transmitted institutional models, how the papal petition system influenced royal chanceries and courts.

Comparative case analysis of England (papal ally, early centralization), France (intermediate), and the Holy Roman Empire (papal enemy, delayed centralization) to demonstrate that where the papacy fragmented authority, central state institutions could not develop, while papal allies were free to centralize (pp. 80–82).

第二章，定量方法：

以100公里×100公里网格单元为分析单位的双向固定效应OLS回归（单元固定效应和年份固定效应）（第62–63页）。固定效应控制了不随时间变化的空间因素（气候、海拔、农业适宜性、距港口距离）和不随空间变化的时间冲击。

样本被分为中世纪（1000–1350年）和早期现代（1500–1800年）两个时期，以捕捉理论预期中教皇影响与世俗影响之间的差异。

主要结果（表2.1，第66页）显示，在中世纪时期的多种模型设定中（模型2–4），教皇冲突与领土碎片化呈统计显著的正相关，且在控制了议会、城市化、公社和神圣罗马帝国成员身份后仍然成立。长子继承制吸收了教皇冲突的效应（模型5），但作者论证长子继承制本身是教会家庭法改革的下游后果，使其成为中介变量而非混淆变量（第65页；附录表A.5的中介分析显示33%的效应通过长子继承制传导）。

在早期现代时期，教皇冲突与新教地位共线性，效应消失（模型7–10）；世俗冲突与碎片化呈正相关，这与战争主义命题的预测相反（第65页）。

报告了Oster (2019年) 敏感性检验（\(\delta = 0 = \beta\)），以评估遗漏变量偏误的风险。中世纪教皇冲突的负值表明，未观测变量需要运行在相反方向才能推翻结果（表2.1，第66页）。

有效性所需的关键假设：

双向固定效应要求教皇冲突与碎片化之间的关系不受未观测到的随时间变化且因单元而异的混淆因素驱动。这是标准的平行趋势类假设。

作者承认反向因果的问题，即教皇可能倾向于攻击已经碎片化的地区，但基于历史论据进行了反驳：教皇针对的是最大的威胁（强大的统治者），而且碎片化是在教皇行动于12世纪全面展开之后才增加的，而非之前（第63页，脚注23）。

“教皇冲突”的操作化在控制了固定效应和控制变量后必须是外生的。由于教皇基于地缘政治威胁选择目标，存在潜在的选择进入处理（selection into treatment）问题。

第三章，定性方法：

对七个政治体和教会的制度发展时间线进行了结构化比较（图3.1, 3.2）。

对具体因果机制进行了过程追踪：萨拉丁什一税如何塑造了英格兰的税收，神职人员如何传递制度模式，教皇请愿制度如何影响了王室文书处和法院。

对英格兰（教皇盟友，早期集权化）、法兰西（中间状态）和神圣罗马帝国（教皇敌人，延迟集权化）进行了比较案例分析，以证明教皇碎片化权威之处中央国家制度无法发展，而教皇盟友则可自由集权（第80–82页）。
