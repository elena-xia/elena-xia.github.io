Davide Cantoni, Jeremiah Dittmar & Noam Yuchtman (2018), “Religious Competition and Reallocation: The Political Economy of Secularization in the Protestant Reformation,” QJE.坎托尼、迪特马尔、尤赫特曼（2018）《宗教竞争与资源再配置：新教改革中世俗化的政治经济学》，QJE。

Dependent variable (DV)

The main dependent variable is the sectoral allocation of economic resources between the religious and secular sectors, three distinct outcome domains. The first is the occupational choice of university graduates, specifically, whether their first job was in the church sector (e.g., priest, monk) or the secular sector (e.g., city councillor, guild master), and further, whether a secular occupation was an administrative one (p. 2040). The second is the human capital investment of university students, specifically, whether the degree obtained was in theology (church-specific) or in a secular field such as the arts, law, or medicine (pp. 2040–2041). The third is the composition of major construction activity at the town level, specifically, whether new construction starts were for religious purposes (e.g., churches, monasteries) or secular purposes (e.g., palaces, administrative buildings, hospitals, merchant halls), and further whether secular construction reflected the interests of secular lords (palaces and administrative buildings) (pp. 2041–2042). Each of these three measures broader process of economic secularization..

主要因变量是经济资源在宗教部门与世俗部门之间的分配，沿三个维度衡量。第一是大学毕业生的职业选择，具体而言，其首份工作是否属于教会部门（如牧师、修士）还是世俗部门（如城市议员、行会会长），以及世俗职业中是否属于行政类职业（第2040页）。第二是大学生的人力资本投资，具体而言，所获学位是神学（教会专用型）还是世俗学科如文科、法学或医学（第2040–2041页）。第三是城镇层面的重大建筑活动的构成，具体而言，新建筑开工是用于宗教目的（如教堂、修道院）还是世俗目的（如宫殿、行政建筑、医院、商业大厅），以及世俗建筑是否反映了世俗领主的利益（宫殿和行政建筑）（第2041–2042页）。三个因变量各自从不同边际操作化了“经济世俗化”的概念，资源从宗教用途向世俗用途的转移。

Independent variable (IV)

At the theoretical level, the main independent variable is the introduction of religious competition during the Protestant Reformation. The authors emphasize that what matters is not Protestant doctrine in the abstract, but the competitive shock created when the Catholic Church’s monopoly was challenged in both the market for salvation and the market for political legitimacy. Empirically, this shock is proxied by whether a territory’s ruler or a university eventually became Protestant, interacted with time relative to the Reformation. Thus, the paper’s core explanatory variable is best described as religious competition, with territorial or university adoption of Protestantism serving as its principal empirical proxy.

在理论层面，本文的主要自变量是宗教改革期间引入的宗教竞争。作者强调，关键并不只是“新教教义”本身，而是天主教会在救赎市场和政治合法性市场上的垄断地位受到挑战这一竞争冲击。实证上，作者则用领地统治者或大学是否最终转为新教，并与宗教改革前后时间相互作用，来代理这一冲击。因此，更准确的说法是：本文的核心解释变量在理论上是宗教竞争，在经验上则主要通过领地/大学的新教化来加以识别。

Stateness

The paper does not study state building as its primary dependent variable, nor does it directly measure state capacity using standard indicators such as taxation, military extraction, or bureaucratic reach. Its central object is economic secularization: a shift in power and resources away from church elites and toward secular authorities, especially territorial lords. However, the strengthening of secular territorial lords at the expense of religious elites is a central finding that speaks directly to the state formation literature. The authors conceptualize the political structure of the Holy Roman Empire as an imperial federation of semi-autonomous principalities, in which territorial lords (Landesherren /ˈlɑːndəsˌhɛrən/) exercised sovereignty over their domains (pp. 2049–2050). In the Holy Roman Empire context, these territorial rulers and their governing apparatus are the relevant political actors most closely corresponding to “the state.”

The expropriation of monastic wealth, the movement of upper-tail human capital into administrative occupations, and the increase in palaces and administrative buildings all suggest an expansion of secular rulers’ power and governing capacity. The authors themselves describe the process as involving both institutional secularization, the strengthening of territorial lords, and allocational secularization, the reallocation of resources toward secular purposes favored by those rulers. But these can be read as historically important implications for state formation, not as direct measures of state building in the narrow fiscal-administrative sense.

The authors explicitly discuss this connection: Cohn (1987, 176) is quoted as observing that the Reformation concentrated previously dispersed functions into the state, with officialdom penetrating “more pervasively than before in towns, villages and families” and ecclesiastical jurisdiction being “swept away” (p. 2058). They also note that “allocational and institutional secularization strengthened territorial rulers and might have contributed to the development of early modern states with greater state capacity” (p. 2092). This places their work in dialogue with the broader state formation literature. In particular, their findings complement Tilly’s (1990) argument that war and extraction drove state building by identifying an additional mechanism, religious competition, through which rulers acquired resources and expanded their administrative apparatus. Their framework also directly engages with Rubin (2017), who argues that the relative independence of European secular rulers from religious legitimation (compared to the Islamic world) was important for Western economic and political development.

本文并不把国家建设作为其首要因变量来直接研究，也没有用税收能力、军事汲取能力、官僚渗透等标准指标直接测量国家能力。它真正的核心对象是经济世俗化：即权力和资源从教会精英向世俗权威、尤其是领地统治者转移。然而，世俗领地领主以宗教精英为代价的权力增强是一项核心发现，直接关涉国家形成文献。作者将神圣罗马帝国的政治结构概念化为一个由半自治公国组成的帝国联邦，其中领地领主（Landesherren /ˈlɑːndəsˌhɛrən/）在其领地行使主权（第2049–2050页）。在神圣罗马帝国的历史语境下，这些领地统治者及其治理装置，是文中最接近“国家”的政治行为者。

修道院财富被领主征收、上层人力资本转向行政职业、以及宫殿和行政建筑增加，都表明世俗统治者的权力和治理能力扩张。作者自己也将这一过程概括为两部分：一是制度性世俗化，即领地统治者相对于教会精英的加强；二是配置性世俗化，即资源向这些统治者偏好的世俗用途再配置。但更准确、更 fair 的表述应是：这些是对国家形成文献有启发性的结果和含义，而不是本文直接测量“国家建设”的主设计。

作者明确讨论了这一联系：引用Cohn (1987, 176)的观察，宗教改革将此前分散的功能集中到国家，官僚机构“比以往更深入地渗透到城镇、村庄和家庭”，教会司法管辖权被“扫除”（第2058页）。他们还指出“配置性世俗化和制度性世俗化加强了领地统治者，可能促进了具有更大国家能力的近代早期国家的发展”（第2092页）。这使他们的工作与更广泛的国家形成文献对话。特别是，他们的发现补充了蒂利(1990)关于战争和汲取推动国家建设的论点，识别出一种额外的机制，宗教竞争，通过该机制统治者获得资源并扩展其行政机构。他们的框架还直接与Rubin (2017)对话，后者论证欧洲世俗统治者相对于宗教合法化的独立性（与伊斯兰世界相比）对西方经济和政治发展至关重要。

Core argument

The core argument is that the introduction of religious competition during the Protestant Reformation produced an important, unintended consequence: a large-scale reallocation of resources from religious to secular purposes, which constituted a fundamental process of economic secularization in early modern Europe. The key analytical innovation is that to understand this process, one must consider two linked markets. The first is the “market for salvation”, in which churches provide religious services to believers, as prior scholarship emphasizes (Ekelund, Hébert, and Tollison 2006). The second is the “market for political legitimacy”, in which religious authorities provide endorsement and legitimation to secular rulers in exchange for temporal concessions of money, land, economic privileges, and political power (pp. 2038–2039).

The argument proceeds in three steps. First, before the Reformation, the Catholic Church held a monopoly in both markets. As a monopolist, it charged a high price: believers paid through tithes, sacramental fees, and indulgences; secular lords paid by granting the Church enormous tax exemptions, land, jurisdictional authority, and the right to appoint clerics to positions of immense power and wealth (pp. 2049–2050). However, the Church’s monopoly power was not unlimited: there was continual bargaining and conflict between secular and religious elites over the distribution of resources, as exemplified by the Investiture Controversy of the eleventh century and ongoing disputes between territorial lords and Church institutions within the Holy Roman Empire (pp. 2050–2051).

Second, the Reformation constituted a competitive shock to both markets simultaneously. In the market for salvation, Protestant reformers offered a lower-cost alternative: “disintermediation” between the believer and God, salvation by faith alone, reduction of sacraments from seven to two, and the abolition of the expensive clerical bureaucracy (p. 2052). In the market for political legitimacy, the entry of a competitor reduced the value of Catholic legitimacy (by questioning Catholic theology and attracting believers away), gave secular rulers the ability to bargain between two providers, and meant that Protestant reformers, who needed protection from secular lords, would accept a lower price in exchange for conferring legitimacy (pp. 2048–2049). Specifically, Protestant theologians provided detailed legal justifications for the expropriation of Catholic Church property by secular lords, based on meticulous citations of Canon and Roman law (p. 2053). The Catholic Church, constrained by doctrinal consistency, could not match this bargain.

Third, the resulting new equilibrium had sweeping implications for resource allocation. With secular lords paying a lower price for legitimacy, they retained more resources. This produced a shift in the occupational composition of the labor force (from church to secular jobs, especially administration), in the human capital investments of forward-looking university students (from theology to secular subjects like law and the arts), and in the sectoral composition of fixed investments (from church construction to secular construction, especially palaces and administrative buildings). These effects were concentrated in Protestant territories but also visible in Catholic territories bordering Protestant ones due to the “intensive margin” of religious competition (pp. 2083–2085).

The authors carefully distinguish two components of the secularization process they document: “institutional secularization”, the strengthening of territorial lords relative to church elites, and “allocational secularization”, the reallocation of economic inputs toward secular purposes, specifically those favored by territorial lords (p. 2045). They emphasize that the Reformation’s secularizing effects were unintended: the reformers’ aim was religious purification, not the enrichment of secular authorities. Tawney (1926, 88) is quoted: the political economy consequences were “without design, and against the intention of most reformers” (p. 2047).

核心论点是，新教改革期间引入的宗教竞争产生了一个重要的、非预期的后果：资源从宗教用途向世俗用途的大规模再配置，构成了近代早期欧洲经济世俗化的基本过程。关键的分析创新在于，要理解这一过程，不仅需要考虑“救赎市场”，教会向信徒提供宗教服务，如先前学术研究（Ekelund, Hébert, and Tollison 2006）所强调的，还必须考虑第二个相互关联的“政治合法性市场”，宗教权威向世俗统治者提供背书和合法化，以换取金钱、土地、经济特权和政治权力的世俗让步（第2038–2039页）。

论证分三步展开。第一，宗教改革之前，天主教会在两个市场中都享有垄断地位。作为垄断者，它收取高昂的价格：信徒通过什一税、圣事费和赎罪券支付；世俗领主则授予教会巨大的免税权、土地、司法管辖权以及任命神职人员至极具权力和财富之职位的权利（第2049–2050页）。然而，教会的垄断权力并非不受限制：世俗精英与宗教精英之间围绕资源分配的讨价还价和冲突持续不断，十一世纪的“叙任权之争”（Investiture Controversy）以及神圣罗马帝国内领地领主与教会机构之间的持续争端即为例证（第2050–2051页）。

第二，宗教改革构成了对两个市场的同时竞争冲击。在救赎市场，新教改革者提供了成本更低的替代方案：信徒与上帝之间的“去中介化”、唯信称义、圣事从七项减至两项，以及废除昂贵的教士官僚体系（第2052页）。在政治合法性市场，竞争者的进入降低了天主教合法性的价值（通过质疑天主教神学并吸引信徒离开），赋予世俗统治者在两个供应商之间讨价还价的能力，而且，关键的是，新教改革者由于需要与世俗领主达成保护协议，愿意以更低的价格提供合法性（第2048–2049页）。具体而言，新教神学家基于对教会法和罗马法的详尽引用，为世俗领主征收天主教会财产提供了详细的法律理据（第2053页）。天主教会受教义一致性约束，无法匹配这一交易。

第三，由此产生的新均衡对资源配置产生了深远影响。世俗领主为合法性支付更低的价格，因而保留了更多资源。这产生了劳动力职业构成的转变（从教会职业转向世俗职业，尤其是行政职业）、前瞻性大学生人力资本投资的转变（从神学转向法学和文科等世俗学科）、以及固定投资部门构成的转变（从教堂建设转向世俗建设，尤其是宫殿和行政建筑）。这些效应集中在新教领地，但也在毗邻新教的天主教领地中可见，这源于宗教竞争的“集约边际”（第2083–2085页）。

作者仔细区分了他们所记录的世俗化过程的两个组成部分：“制度性世俗化”，领地领主相对于教会精英的加强，和“配置性世俗化”，经济投入向世俗用途的再配置，特别是向领地领主偏好的用途的再配置（第2045页）。他们强调宗教改革的世俗化效应是非预期的：改革者的目标是宗教净化，而非世俗权威的富裕化。引用Tawney (1926, 88)的话：这些政治经济后果“既非出于设计，也违背了大多数改革者的意图”（第2047页）。

Mechanisms

The authors propose a mechanism chain centered on the interaction between religious competition and politics. The Reformation introduced a religious competitor to the Catholic Church, which lowered the price of salvation for believers and changed the terms on which secular rulers could obtain political legitimacy. As Catholic legitimacy became less valuable and Protestant reformers were more willing to grant rulers control over church resources, the bargaining position of territorial lords improved.

The paper provides especially strong evidence for the next step in the chain: secular rulers gained wealth and authority through monastery closures and the expropriation of church property. This in turn is argued to have changed resource allocation across the broader economy. On the labor-market side, enriched rulers demanded more administrative personnel, while Protestant theology reduced demand for clerical labor and weakened incentives to pursue monastic careers. On the education side, forward-looking students responded to these changing returns by shifting away from theology and toward more secular subjects, especially law and the arts. On the fixed-investment side, newly empowered rulers increasingly directed resources toward palaces and administrative buildings.

A fair reading, however, is that the paper directly documents monastery expropriation and sectoral reallocation much more strongly than it directly measures the intermediate object of a changing “price” in the market for political legitimacy. Thus, the paper’s mechanism is best described as a well-supported historical-political economy account, rather than a fully observed causal chain in which every intermediate link is separately measured.

Mechanism 1: The Competitive Shock to the Market for Legitimacy. The Reformation introduced a new provider of religiously derived political legitimacy. Protestant reformers, needing secular protection and lacking the institutional infrastructure of the Catholic Church, were willing to confer legitimacy on secular rulers at a lower price. This lower price took the concrete form of legal justifications for the expropriation of Catholic Church property. Protestant theologians, described as “the technicians of religious legitimacy” (Ocker 2006, 13; cited at p. 2058), provided memoranda justifying lords’ seizure of monastic wealth based on Canon and Roman law (p. 2053). The Catholic Church, constrained by doctrinal consistency, could not offer comparable concessions without undermining its own theological foundations (p. 2039).

Mechanism 2: The Transfer of Wealth and Political Power. The lower price of legitimacy enabled massive wealth transfers from the Catholic Church to secular lords. The closure and expropriation of monasteries was the primary vehicle. Historical evidence documents significant enrichment in 16 of 18 Protestant territories studied (Table II, p. 2059). The scale was enormous: in Hesse /hɛs/, Landgrave Philipp /ˈlɑːntɡrɑːf ˈfɪlɪp/ received annual revenues equivalent to one-seventh of total state revenues and around 1,000 person-years of skilled wages; overall, 40% of monastic wealth in Hesse went to the ruler (p. 2057). In Brandenburg /ˈbrɑːndənbɜːrɡ/, monasteries paid 300,000 guilders to retain their privileges (p. 2057). This wealth transfer also carried political power: secular lords gained jurisdictional authority previously exercised by ecclesiastical courts, and monastic lands came under direct secular administration (p. 2058).

Mechanism 3: Labor Market Effects, Demand Side. Enriched and empowered lords demanded more skilled labor for administration of their expanded territories and the management of newly acquired properties. An ordinance from Württemberg /ˈvʏrtəmbɛrk/ from 1546 explicitly notes the need for men “to serve in preaching offices, governments, temporal posts, [and] administrative offices” (Strauss 1988; cited at p. 2060). Simultaneously, the simplified Protestant theology reduced demand for clerical labor: fewer sacraments meant fewer priests were needed, and the abolition of monasticism eliminated an entire category of church employment (pp. 2059–2060).

Mechanism 4: Labor Market Effects, Supply Side. The Reformation also affected labor supply. Protestant theology “rebutted the most compelling reason to become a monk or a nun, to save one’s soul and the souls of others” (Ocker 2010, 62; cited at p. 2060), leading to widespread attrition in monasteries and reducing the supply of labor to the religious sector.

Mechanism 5: Forward-Looking Human Capital Investment. Rational, forward-looking university students perceived the sectoral shift in labor demand and adjusted their human capital investments accordingly. The declining returns to church-specific human capital (theology) induced a shift toward secular subjects (law, the arts, medicine) that prepared students for careers in public administration and the private sector (pp. 2060–2061). This mechanism is supported by data showing that theology graduates were overwhelmingly employed in the church sector (90%) compared to non-theology graduates (53%), while arts and law graduates were more than twice as likely to take administrative jobs as others (Table III, p. 2071).

Mechanism 6: Fixed Capital Investment as a Summary Statistic. The increase in secular lords’ resources and power was reflected in patterns of large-scale urban construction, which the authors treat as a summary statistic for the allocation of bundles of resources embodying land, physical, financial, and human capital. Empowered lords used their new wealth to construct palaces (signaling power and providing consumption) and administrative buildings (projecting expanded authority) (p. 2061). The authors note that this effect appeared with a lag, reflecting the insecurity of lords’ property rights claims before the mid-sixteenth century: full consolidation of expropriated wealth occurred only after the Schmalkaldic War (1546–1547) and the Peace of Augsburg /ˈaʊɡsbɜːrɡ/ (1555) (pp. 2053, 2061).

Mechanism 7: The Intensive Margin of Religious Competition. The model also predicts differential effects within Catholic territories depending on their proximity to Protestant regions. Catholic territories bordering Protestant ones experienced greater competitive pressure, the Catholic Church had to concede more to secular lords in these border regions to prevent defection to Protestantism. This implies a gradient in secularization effects, with the largest Protestant-Catholic differences appearing between Protestant territories and Catholic territories far from Protestant borders (pp. 2083–2085).

作者提出了一条以宗教竞争与政治互动为核心的机制链。宗教改革为天主教会引入了一个新的宗教竞争者，这不仅降低了信徒获得救赎的成本，也改变了世俗统治者获得政治合法性的条件。随着天主教合法性的价值下降，而新教改革者更愿意向世俗统治者让渡对教会资源的控制，领地统治者在与宗教精英的讨价还价中获得了更有利的位置。

本文对机制链中下一步的证据尤其强：世俗统治者通过修道院关闭和教产征收获得了财富与权力。作者进一步论证，这种变化带来了更广泛的资源再配置。就劳动力市场而言，财富和权力增强的统治者对行政人员的需求上升，而新教神学则降低了对教士劳动的需求，并削弱了人们进入修道生活的动机。就教育投资而言，前瞻性的学生因预期回报变化而从神学转向更世俗的学科，尤其是法学和文科。就固定投资而言，权力增强的统治者越来越多地将资源投入宫殿和行政建筑。

但更 fair 的写法应承认：本文最直接、最有力地记录的是修道院征收和资源再配置本身；至于“政治合法性市场中的价格变化”这一中介对象，则更多是由历史材料和结果模式加以支持，而不是被直接测量出来。 因此，更准确的表述是：这是一套有充分支持的历史政治经济学机制叙述，而不是每一个中间环节都被单独观测和识别的完整因果链。

机制一：合法性市场的竞争冲击。 宗教改革引入了一个新的宗教来源政治合法性供应者。新教改革者需要世俗保护且缺乏天主教会的制度基础设施，愿意以更低的价格向世俗统治者提供合法性。这一较低价格以征收天主教会财产的法律理据的具体形式呈现。被描述为“宗教合法性的技术员”（Ocker 2006, 13；引于第2058页）的新教神学家提供了基于教会法和罗马法为领主征收修道院财富辩护的备忘录（第2053页）。天主教会受教义一致性约束，无法提供相当的让步而不损害其自身的神学基础（第2039页）。

机制二：财富和政治权力的转移。 合法性的较低价格使天主教会向世俗领主的大规模财富转移成为可能。修道院的关闭和征收是主要载体。历史证据记录了所研究的18个新教领地中16个的领主显著致富（表II，第2059页）。规模巨大：在黑森 /hɛs/，领伯菲利普（Landgrave Philipp /ˈlɑːntɡrɑːf ˈfɪlɪp/）的年收入相当于国家总收入的七分之一和约1,000个技术工人年工资；总体而言，黑森修道院财富的40%归于统治者（第2057页）。在勃兰登堡（Brandenburg /ˈbrɑːndənbɜːrɡ/），修道院支付了30万古尔登以保留特权（第2057页）。这一财富转移同时携带了政治权力：世俗领主获得了此前由教会法庭行使的司法管辖权，修道院土地被纳入直接的世俗行政管辖（第2058页）。

机制三：劳动力市场效应，需求侧。 富裕和权力增强的领主需要更多技术劳动力来管理其扩大的领地和新获得的资产。1546年符腾堡（Württemberg /ˈvʏrtəmbɛrk/）的一项法令明确指出需要人员“在传教职位、政府部门、世俗职位和行政机关中服务”（Strauss 1988；引于第2060页）。同时，简化的新教神学减少了对教士劳动力的需求：更少的圣事意味着需要更少的牧师，修道主义的废除消除了整个类别的教会就业（第2059–2060页）。

机制六：固定资本投资作为综合统计量。 世俗领主资源和权力的增加反映在大规模城市建筑的模式中，作者将其视为包含土地、实物资本、金融资本和人力资本等资源束配置的综合统计量。权力增强的领主用新获得的财富建造宫殿（象征权力并提供消费价值）和行政建筑（投射扩大的权威）（第2061页）。作者注意到这一效应有时滞，反映了十六世纪中期之前领主对被征收财产产权主张的不确定性：被征收财富的完全巩固仅在施马尔卡尔登战争（1546–1547年）和奥格斯堡和约（Augsburg /ˈaʊɡsbɜːrɡ/，1555年）之后才实现（第2053页、第2061页）。

机制七：宗教竞争的集约边际。 该模型还预测天主教领地内部因与新教地区的距离不同而产生差异化效应。毗邻新教的天主教领地经历了更大的竞争压力，天主教会必须在这些边境地区向世俗领主做出更多让步以防止倒向新教。这意味着世俗化效应存在梯度，最大的新教-天主教差异出现在新教领地与远离新教边界的天主教领地之间（第2083–2085页）。

Data & methods

Data

The paper assembles an unusually rich set of microdata from multiple primary and secondary historical sources, covering the German-speaking lands of the Holy Roman Empire from approximately 1470 to 1600.

Data on University Graduates’ Degrees and Careers. The primary source for individual-level data on university graduates through 1550 is the Repertorium Academicum Germanicum (RAG; Schwinges and Hesse 2015), an online database collecting the universe of recipients of academic degrees from 16 German universities (pp. 2062–2063). The RAG provides information on each degree recipient’s subject (arts, law, medicine, or theology), year, and, for approximately one-quarter of graduates between 1480 and 1550, their careers. Career information includes over 400 occupational titles, classified into church and secular sectors using the Thesaurus Professionum (Marburg Universität 2015) (p. 2063). To extend coverage beyond 1550, the authors hand-collected university-level degree data from historical registries for the period 1540–1600 (p. 2062). The resulting datasets include 10,022 individual degree observations for the human capital investment analysis and 2,408 individuals with career data for the occupational choice analysis.

Data on Monastery Closure. Data on 3,094 monasteries are gathered from Jürgensmeier and Schwerdtfeger (2005–2008), including location, foundation date, and closure date. For each of over 2,200 towns in the Deutsches Städtebuch, the authors calculate the number of monasteries within 25 km open in each year (pp. 2054–2055).

Data on Construction Activity. Several thousand unique major construction events at the town level are hand-coded from the Deutsches Städtebuch, a comprehensive multi-volume reference work on German cities and towns. Each construction event is coded by location, start date, and sector (church or secular), with secular construction further disaggregated into administrative, palaces, welfare, economic, military, and other categories. The analysis covers over 2,000 towns (p. 2064). Construction data are aggregated to territory-by-decade cells for the formal regression analysis.

Assignment of Towns and Territories to Religious Denominations. Towns are assigned to territorial lords using the Euratlas mapping (Nüssli 2008) for 1500, and religion is coded using Cantoni (2012). This produces 983 towns in eventually Protestant territories and 558 towns in Catholic territories (p. 2055).

Geographic Coverage and Unit of Analysis. The analysis covers the German-speaking lands of the Holy Roman Empire. The unit of analysis varies: individual graduates for occupational choice and human capital investment; territory-by-decade for construction activity; and town-by-year for monastery counts. There are 35 territories in the construction regressions (p. 2079).

本文汇集了来自多种一手和二手历史资料的异常丰富的微观数据集，覆盖约1470年至1600年神圣罗马帝国的德语地区。

大学毕业生学位和职业数据。 1550年之前大学毕业生个体层面数据的主要来源是Repertorium Academicum Germanicum（RAG；Schwinges and Hesse 2015），这是一个收集16所德国大学所有学位获得者信息的在线数据库（第2062–2063页）。RAG提供每位学位获得者的学科（文科、法学、医学或神学）、年份，以及，对于1480至1550年间约四分之一的毕业生，他们的职业信息。职业信息包括400多个职业头衔，使用Thesaurus Professionum（马尔堡大学 2015）分类为教会和世俗部门（第2063页）。为将覆盖范围扩展到1550年以后，作者从历史注册簿中手工收集了1540–1600年期间的大学层面学位数据（第2062页）。由此得到的数据集包括人力资本投资分析的10,022个个体学位观测值和职业选择分析的2,408个有职业数据的个体。

修道院关闭数据。 3,094个修道院的数据来自

Jürgensmeier and Schwerdtfeger (2005–2008)，包括位置、建立日期和关闭日期。对于Deutsches Städtebuch

中2,200多个城镇中的每一个，作者计算了每年25公里范围内开放的修道院数量（第2054–2055页）。

建筑活动数据。 数千个城镇层面独特的重大建筑事件从Deutsches Städtebuch，一部关于德国城镇的综合性多卷参考著作，中手工编码。每个建筑事件按位置、开工日期和部门（教会或世俗）编码，世俗建筑进一步细分为行政、宫殿、福利、经济、军事和其他类别。分析覆盖2,000多个城镇（第2064页）。建筑数据在正式回归分析中聚合为领地-十年单元。

城镇和领地的宗教归属分配。 城镇使用1500年的Euratlas地图（Nüssli 2008）分配给领地领主，宗教编码使用Cantoni (2012)。这产生了最终成为新教领地的983个城镇和天主教领地的558个城镇（第2055页）。

地理覆盖范围和分析单位。 分析覆盖神圣罗马帝国的德语地区。分析单位不同：职业选择和人力资本投资为个体毕业生；建筑活动为领地-十年；修道院数量为城镇-年。建筑回归中有35个领地（第2079页）。

Methods

The paper employs difference-in-differences (DID) estimation as its primary identification strategy, implemented in three separate empirical contexts with consistent logic.

Occupational Choice. The authors estimate individual-level linear probability models (OLS) with university and decade fixed effects. The key specification is a flexible DID model (equation 1, p. 2066) where the outcome is a binary indicator for whether a graduate’s first job was in the church sector, and the explanatory variables of interest are interactions between an “eventually Protestant university” dummy and decade dummies, with the 1510–1519 decade as the omitted reference. They also estimate a more aggregated specification (equation 2, p. 2067) with three time periods (1480–1509, 1510–1519, 1520–1549) to produce a formal pre-trend test and a pooled post-Reformation estimate. Standard errors are clustered at the university-by-decade level. The sample comprises 2,408 individuals with recorded occupations.

Human Capital Investment. An analogous individual-level DID model (equations 3 and 4, pp. 2073) is estimated with the outcome being a binary indicator for a theology degree (or, in the complementary analysis, a law or arts degree). The same university and decade fixed effects, Protestant-university interaction structure, and clustering scheme are used. The sample comprises 10,022 degree observations.

Construction Activity. The analysis shifts to the territory-by-decade level. Counts of construction events by sector are regressed on territory and decade fixed effects, with the key explanatory variables being interactions between an “eventually Protestant territory” dummy and decade dummies (equations 5 and 6, pp. 2077–2078). The omitted reference decade is again 1510–1519. An aggregated specification uses four time periods: 1480–1509, 1510–1519, 1520–1549, and 1550–1599, with the last period separated to capture the consolidation of lords’ property rights after the Schmalkaldic War. Standard errors are clustered at the territory level. There are 35 territories.

Robustness and Identification. The core identifying assumption is the parallel trends assumption: absent the Reformation, Protestant and Catholic territories (or universities) would have followed similar trajectories in the outcomes of interest. The authors support this assumption through several strategies. First, the flexible DID specifications allow visual inspection of pre-Reformation trends, and in nearly all analyses, the Protestant-Catholic differences before 1517 are small and statistically insignificant, with no differential trend toward the post-Reformation patterns (pp. 2067–2068, 2073–2074, 2078–2082). Second, for construction activity, the authors include very flexible controls for pre-Reformation construction levels, interactions between a territory’s total construction stock up to the 1470s and a full set of decade dummies, as well as interactions between each territory’s construction level in each pre-Reformation decade and a full set of decade dummies, which absorb all pre-Reformation level differences (p. 2082). Third, the authors conduct a heterogeneity analysis exploiting variation in the “intensity” of religious competition, comparing Protestant territories with Catholic border regions (high competition) versus Catholic interior regions (low competition) (pp. 2083–2085). Fourth, to address concerns about time-varying confounders that might simultaneously drive both the adoption of Protestantism and secularization, the authors present three territorial case studies, Brandenburg /ˈbrɑːndənbɜːrɡ/, the Duchy of Saxony /ˈsæksəni/, and the Duchy of Württemberg /ˈvʏrtəmbɛrk/, where the timing of religious change was plausibly exogenous because it was determined by unanticipated changes in rulers (e.g., the death of a Catholic duke succeeded by a Protestant heir) rather than by underlying economic conditions (pp. 2087–2091). Fifth, the authors demonstrate that results are not driven by large cities, showing similar patterns in small towns (Figure XII, p. 2088).

Key Assumptions for Validity. For the DID estimates to identify a causal effect of the Reformation, several assumptions must hold. (i) Parallel trends: absent the Reformation, outcomes would have evolved similarly in Protestant and Catholic regions. (ii) No anticipation: agents did not change behavior before the Reformation in anticipation of it. (iii) SUTVA (stable unit treatment value assumption): a territory’s treatment (adoption of Protestantism) did not affect outcomes in other territories. The authors explicitly acknowledge a potential violation of SUTVA through the “intensive margin” of religious competition, Catholic border regions were affected by Protestantism, and this motivates the heterogeneity analysis in Figure X. (iv) Conditional exogeneity of treatment timing: the case studies serve to address this concern for a subset of territories.

本文以双重差分法（DID）作为主要识别策略，在三个独立的实证环境中以一致的逻辑实施。

职业选择。 作者估计了包含大学和十年固定效应的个体层面线性概率模型（OLS）。关键设定是灵活的DID模型（方程1，第2066页），其中结果变量是毕业生首份工作是否在教会部门的二元指标，关键解释变量是“最终成为新教大学”虚拟变量与十年虚拟变量的交互项，以1510–1519年十年为省略的参照期。他们还估计了更聚合的设定（方程2，第2067页），使用三个时期（1480–1509、1510–1519、1520–1549）以产生正式的前趋势检验和汇总的改革后估计。标准误在大学-十年层面聚类。样本包含2,408个有记录职业的个体。

人力资本投资。 估计了类似的个体层面DID模型（方程3和4，第2073页），结果变量为神学学位的二元指标（或在互补分析中，法学或文科学位的二元指标）。使用相同的大学和十年固定效应、新教大学交互项结构和聚类方案。样本包含10,022个学位观测值。

建筑活动。 分析转向领地-十年层面。按部门的建筑事件计数对领地和十年固定效应回归，关键解释变量为“最终成为新教领地”虚拟变量与十年虚拟变量的交互项（方程5和6，第2077–2078页）。省略的参照十年仍为1510–1519年。聚合设定使用四个时期：1480–1509、1510–1519、1520–1549和1550–1599，最后一个时期单独列出以捕捉施马尔卡尔登战争后领主产权的巩固。标准误在领地层面聚类。共有35个领地。

稳健性与识别。 核心识别假设是平行趋势假设：如果没有宗教改革，新教和天主教领地（或大学）在感兴趣的结果上会遵循相似的轨迹。作者通过几种策略支持这一假设。第一，灵活的DID设定允许目视检查改革前趋势，在几乎所有分析中，1517年前的新教-天主教差异都很小且统计上不显著，没有向改革后模式的差异化趋势（第2067–2068页、第2073–2074页、第2078–2082页）。第二，对于建筑活动，作者加入了非常灵活的改革前建筑水平控制，领地截至1470年代的总建筑存量与十年虚拟变量全集的交互项，以及每个领地在每个改革前十年的建筑水平与十年虚拟变量全集的交互项，这吸收了所有改革前水平差异（第2082页）。第三，作者进行了异质性分析，利用宗教竞争“强度”的变异，比较新教领地与天主教边境地区（高竞争）和天主教内陆地区（低竞争）（第2083–2085页）。第四，为解决可能同时驱动新教采纳和世俗化的时变混淆因素的担忧，作者提出了三个领地案例研究，勃兰登堡（Brandenburg /ˈbrɑːndənbɜːrɡ/）、萨克森公国（Duchy of Saxony /ˈsæksəni/）和符腾堡公国（Duchy of Württemberg /ˈvʏrtəmbɛrk/），在这些地方，宗教变革的时机可以被合理地认为是外生的，因为它是由统治者的意外变更（例如天主教公爵去世后由新教继承人继位）而非潜在的经济条件决定的（第2087–2091页）。第五，作者证明结果并非由大城市驱动，在小城镇中显示了相似的模式（图XII，第2088页）。

有效性的关键假设。 要使DID估计识别宗教改革的因果效应，必须满足几个假设。(i) 平行趋势：如果没有宗教改革，新教和天主教地区的结果会有相似的演变。(ii) 无预期：行为人没有在预期宗教改革的情况下提前改变行为。(iii) SUTVA（稳定的单位处理值假设）：一个领地的处理（采纳新教）不会影响其他领地的结果。作者明确承认SUTVA可能通过宗教竞争的“集约边际”被违反，天主教边境地区受到新教的影响，这激发了图X的异质性分析。(iv) 处理时机的条件外生性：案例研究旨在为领地子集解决这一担忧。
